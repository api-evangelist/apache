<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Conversation</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. How did you first get involved with Apache?</strong><strong><br /></strong><strong>A:</strong> In 2004, while working at a public research lab in Southern France, I was responsible for implementing complex algorithms in C, C++, and Java. Rather than reinventing the wheel, I sought out existing open source code to integrate into my projects. This led me to contribute improvements back to those libraries, marking the beginning of my long-term involvement with The Apache Software Foundation.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. What Apache projects or open source initiatives are you currently focused on?<br />A. </strong>Over the past several years, I have pivoted my focus toward the big data ecosystem. I am particularly drawn to the architectural challenges of distributed systems—specifically, how to maintain consistency and availability at scale. I find the evolution of distributed querying engines fascinating, as they bridge the gap between massive, unstructured datasets and real-time, actionable insights. That&#8217;s why I&#8217;m involved in Apache Iceberg, Apache Polaris, Apache Arrow, and Apache Beam, among others.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I&#8217;m still very involved in Apache ActiveMQ, Apache Camel, and Apache Karaf because I see a lot of opportunities in this integration area. This integration/middleware domain is not dead at all; I think we have a lot of opportunities here.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. What excites you most about the work happening in the Apache community right now?</strong><strong><br /></strong><strong>A. </strong>I see more collaborations across the Apache communities. We see a lot of collaborations between the different projects. Lakehouse Day and Community Over Code Con are two events that showcase how ASF projects work together to create their own ecosystem(s).</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. What challenges or opportunities are you seeing in open source today?</strong><br /><strong>A. </strong>Even if it&#8217;s not easy, I think The ASF is doing a great job around project governance. We are finding the right balance between neutrality and vendor contributions.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The rise of AI presents a tremendous opportunity for the Foundation. The ASF’s community over code ethos gives us a unique and credible voice to lead the AI conversation in a responsible, ethical way. Following the release of our <a href="https://www.apache.org/legal/generative-tooling.html#:~:text=The%20terms%20and%20conditions%20of,machine%20parsable%20Tooling%2DProvenance%20file.">Generative Tooling Guidelines</a>, the ASF is well positioned to play a major role in shaping the open source AI ecosystem. I am excited about what lies ahead and committed to helping the Foundation seize this moment. I see The ASF playing a key role in responsible AI guidance.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. What technology or industry trends do you think will shape the future of open source?</strong><br /><strong>A. </strong>We are moving beyond simple AI coding assistants. The new trend is Agentic AI—autonomous open source agents that don&#8217;t just suggest code but independently manage entire workflows (reading repositories, planning changes, running tests, and deploying products).</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">With AI handling the boilerplate, the barrier between &#8220;idea&#8221; and &#8220;software&#8221; has collapsed. Domain experts (scientists, doctors, etc.) are increasingly becoming &#8220;developers&#8221; by using natural language to orchestrate open source agents. While AI helps developers, it also creates &#8220;AI slop&#8221;—a flood of low-quality automated pull requests. Open source maintainers are increasingly using AI defensively to triage, label, and filter these contributions. That&#8217;s where The ASF has a huge opportunity to shape a responsible AI future for open source.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Also, regulations like the EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA), which takes effect in late 2026, are forcing the open source community to treat security as a legal mandate rather than a best practice. We are seeing the rise of &#8220;digital provenance&#8221; tools. These map out not just what code is in a project, but who wrote it, where they are located, and how risk is concentrated across contributors.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Security is shifting from manual checklists to “continuous threat exposure management (CTEM). Organizations are now using AI-driven scanners to monitor their entire &#8220;nth-party&#8221; vendor ecosystem in real-time.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. The Apache Way emphasizes community over code. Why does that principle matter? Can you think of an example of why that principle is mission critical for a project? </strong><strong><br /></strong><strong>A. </strong>In the world of open source, code can become obsolete, buggy, or displaced by newer technologies. If a project is built on a code-first mentality, it often relies on a few &#8220;hero&#8221; developers. If those developers leave, the project dies.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And with AI, the code is not where the value is: the design, principles, and features are the game changers.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">By prioritizing community over code, the ASF ensures:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Sustainability &amp; Longevity: </strong>A diverse community ensures that when one person leaves, others are there to step in.</li>



<li><strong>Quality Through Consensus: </strong>Decisions are made through active voting or &#8220;lazy consensus,&#8221; ensuring that various use cases (and potential bugs) are considered before code is merged.</li>



<li><strong>Legal &amp; Intellectual Safety</strong>: A structured community ensures that all contributions are legally vetted, protecting users from patent or copyright issues.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Q. What advice would you give someone who wants to start contributing to an Apache project?</strong><strong><br /></strong><strong>A. </strong>First, find your perfect home: find the project and community you like. If you personally use the project, it&#8217;s easier to be involved. Then start by observing the community via its mailing list and non-code contributions. You will likely be able to identify someone who can help you as a mentor. Then choose a tiny improvement for your first pull request, and work to make your submission effective and accurate. So, it takes time and effort. I often advise newcomers to identify a mentor. It helps them to ramp up quickly in any community.</p>
